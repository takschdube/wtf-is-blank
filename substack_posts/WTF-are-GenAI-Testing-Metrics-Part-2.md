Happy Veterans Day! (yes, I&#8217;m in the US AND I&#8217;m a day late)Quick recap: Last week we covered retrieval metrics - Hit Rate, Recall, NDCG, all that good stuff. If you missed it, go read it first. Seriously. You can&#8217;t evaluate generation if your retrieval is broken. Assuming your retrieval doesn&#8217;t suck (NDCG > 0.7, MRR > 0.7, feeling good), now we make sure your AI isn&#8217;t confidently making stuff up. Let&#8217;s talk about generation metrics. Or: how to know if your AI is lying to you.Part 2: Generation Metrics (Is the Answer Good?)Okay, your retrieval works. You&#8217;re getting the right documents. Now let&#8217;s make sure your AI isn&#8217;t doing creative writing when you asked for facts.Faithfulness / Groundedness: The &#8220;Did It Make Stuff Up?&#8221; MetricThis is the big one. The liability metric. The &#8220;please don&#8217;t hallucinate in front of customers&#8221; metric.What it measures: Percentage of claims in the generated answer that are supported by the retrieved context.How to calculate:Break answer into atomic claimsFor each claim, check if it&#8217;s supported by retrieved docsFaithfulness = (Supported claims) / (Total claims)Example:Answer: &#8220;Our refund policy allows returns within 30 days for unused products.&#8221;Claims: [&#8221;refund policy allows returns&#8221;, &#8220;within 30 days&#8221;, &#8220;for unused products&#8221;]If context only mentions 30 days and returns, but not &#8220;unused&#8221;: 2/3 = 67% faithfulThat&#8217;s a D minus. And in production, a D minus means someone&#8217;s getting a refund they shouldn&#8217;t.What&#8217;s good?Faithfulness > 85% = okay for prototypingFaithfulness > 92% = good for productionFaithfulness > 95% = actually ship thisWhy it matters: Below 90% faithful = your AI is hallucinating. That&#8217;s not a feature, that&#8217;s a bug with legal consequences.How to measure at scale: Use LLM-as-judge (GPT-5 or Claude) to score faithfulness automatically. Yes, using AI to judge AI. Yes, it&#8217;s weird. Yes, it works. The correlation with human judgment is around 0.85-0.90, which beats paying humans to read 10,000 answers.The typical prompt:Given the context and the answer, identify if all claims in the answer are supported by the context. Rate faithfulness from 0-1.Context: [retrieved documents]Answer: [generated answer]The catch: LLM-as-judge costs money. Every evaluation is an API call. Budget accordingly.Answer Relevance: The &#8220;Did It Actually Answer the Question?&#8221; MetricWhat it measures: How well does the answer address the question?How to calculate:Embedding similarity between question and answer (cosine similarity)LLM-as-judge scoring (0-5 scale)What&#8217;s good?Cosine similarity > 0.7 = okay (captures topic)Cosine similarity > 0.8 = good (clearly related)LLM-as-judge score > 4/5 = goodWhy it matters: High faithfulness but low relevance = technically correct but useless.Example:User: &#8220;How do I request a refund?&#8221;AI: &#8220;Our refund policy was updated in 2024 to comply with new regulations.&#8221;Faithful? Yes. Helpful? No.This is the AI equivalent of &#8220;Well, actually...&#8221; Nobody likes that guy. Don&#8217;t be that guy.The tradeoff: Embedding similarity is fast and cheap. LLM-as-judge is slow and expensive but more accurate. Use embedding similarity for continuous monitoring, LLM-as-judge for deep dives.You may be wondering, what is cosine similarity. I got too lazy to type the LaTEX but here is the screenshot from Wikipedia:Context Relevance: The &#8220;Did We Give It the Right Docs?&#8221; MetricWhat it measures: Percentage of retrieved documents that are actually useful for answering the question.The formula:Context Relevance = (Useful docs) / (Retrieved docs)How to measure: LLM-as-judge rates each retrieved doc as useful/not useful for the question.What&#8217;s good?Context Relevance > 0.4 = okay (at least some good docs)Context Relevance > 0.6 = good (mostly good docs)Context Relevance > 0.8 = excellent (tight, relevant context)Why it matters: Low context relevance = you&#8217;re wasting tokens and confusing the model with junk. Also drives up costs.Every irrelevant doc you stuff into the context window is:Costing you money (tokens aren&#8217;t free)Distracting the modelIncreasing latencyMaking your boss ask why the API bill is so highThe reality: If Context Relevance is below 0.5, go back and fix your retrieval. You&#8217;re retrieving too much garbage. This metric is often the canary in the coal mine for broken retrieval.Context Recall: The &#8220;Did We Get All the Info Needed?&#8221; MetricWhat it measures: Whether the retrieved context contains all the information needed to answer the question.How to calculate:Break the ground truth answer into claimsCheck what percentage of those claims can be attributed to the retrieved contextContext Recall = (Attributable claims) / (Total claims in ground truth)What&#8217;s good?Context Recall > 0.8 = goodContext Recall > 0.9 = excellentWhy it matters: High context recall means your retrieval is actually getting the necessary information. Low context recall means you&#8217;re missing critical docs.The catch: This requires ground truth answers, which means human labeling. Use this for test sets, not production monitoring.RAGAS Score: The &#8220;All-in-One RAG Metric&#8221;For people who want one number to rule them all.RAGAS combines multiple metrics into one score:FaithfulnessAnswer RelevanceContext RelevanceContext RecallThe formula:RAGAS = Harmonic mean of [Faithfulness, Answer Relevance,                           Context Relevance, Context Recall]See below if you don&#8217;t know how to take a harmonic mean of something:The harmonic mean punishes low outliers - if one metric is terrible, your RAGAS score tanks.What&#8217;s good?RAGAS > 0.6 = okayRAGAS > 0.7 = goodRAGAS > 0.8 = excellent (or you&#8217;re overfitting to your test set)Why it matters: One number to track. Easier than juggling 10 metrics. Your boss can understand it. Your dashboard looks cleaner.Use RAGAS for monitoring. Use individual metrics for debugging. When RAGAS drops, you need to know which component broke. (It&#8217;s usually faithfulness or context relevance.)The Metrics You Thought You Needed (But Probably Don&#8217;t)BLEU/ROUGE (The &#8220;N-gram Overlap&#8221; Metrics)What they measure: How much the generated answer overlaps with a reference answer (word-level or n-gram level).Why they suck for RAG:&#8220;Our 30-day return policy applies&#8221; vs &#8220;Returns are accepted within one month&#8221;BLEU score: 0.15 (terrible!)Human evaluation: Both perfect &#10003;These metrics were designed for machine translation where exact phrasing matters. Your RAG system isn&#8217;t translating French. Stop using translation metrics.When to use them: Don&#8217;t. Seriously. Unless you&#8217;re actually doing translation or need exact phrasing (you don&#8217;t).Exception: If you&#8217;re testing a model that needs to follow strict templates (legal docs, medical codes), ROUGE-L can catch format deviations. But that&#8217;s it. That&#8217;s the only exception. Stop asking if there are others.BERTScore (The &#8220;Semantic Similarity&#8221; Metric)What it measures: Semantic similarity between generated and reference answers using embeddings.What&#8217;s good?BERTScore > 0.85 = similar meaningBERTScore > 0.90 = very closeWhy it&#8217;s better than BLEU: Catches semantic equivalence even with different words. &#8220;30 days&#8221; vs &#8220;one month&#8221; = high BERTScore. Finally, a metric that understands synonyms.The catch: You need reference answers. That means human labeling. That means budget. That means someone has to write 500 &#8220;correct&#8221; answers for your test set. Good luck getting that prioritized.When to use it: For regression testing (did your changes break quality?) or benchmarking against gold-standard answers. Not for day-to-day monitoring unless you&#8217;re fancy.Perplexity (The &#8220;How Surprised Is the Model?&#8221; Metric)What it measures: How confident is the language model in the generated text?Lower perplexity = more confident = more &#8220;natural&#8221;What&#8217;s good?Depends heavily on the modelCompare to baseline, not absolute numbersWhy it&#8217;s overrated: Low perplexity &#8800; correct answer. The model can be very confident and very wrong. In fact, the most confident wrong answers have great perplexity scores.It&#8217;s the AI equivalent of &#8220;I&#8217;m not saying I&#8217;m right, I&#8217;m just saying I&#8217;m very sure about this incorrect thing.&#8221;When to use it: Detecting gibberish or broken outputs. Not for measuring correctness. If your outputs look like &#8220;hjksdh jksdfh kljsdf&#8221;, perplexity will catch that. If your outputs look like confident lies, it won&#8217;t.The Testing Stack (How to Actually Implement This)Level 1: Retrieval Testing (Build This First)Don&#8217;t touch generation until retrieval works. This is like building a house - if the foundation is broken, who cares about the paint color?Example: test_queries = [    (&#8221;What&#8217;s the refund policy?&#8221;, [doc_id_1, doc_id_5]),    (&#8221;How do I cancel?&#8221;, [doc_id_3, doc_id_7, doc_id_12]),    (&#8221;Can I return opened products?&#8221;, [doc_id_1, doc_id_5, doc_id_8]),    # ... 50-100 test cases (yes, you need that many)]for query, relevant_docs in test_queries:    results = retriever.search(query, k=10)    recall = calculate_recall(results, relevant_docs)    ndcg = calculate_ndcg(results, relevant_docs)        assert recall >= 0.7, f&#8221;Recall too low: {recall}&#8221;    assert ndcg >= 0.6, f&#8221;NDCG too low: {ndcg}&#8221;If these assertions fail: Fix your embeddings, fix your chunking, fix your vector DB setup. Don&#8217;t proceed to generation. You&#8217;re building on quicksand.How to build your test set:Sample real user queries (or make realistic ones)Manually identify which docs are relevant for each queryGrade relevance on a 0-3 scale for NDCGStart with 50 queries, grow to 100+Real talk: This is boring work. Do it anyway. A good test set is worth its weight in gold. Or at least worth the hours you&#8217;ll save debugging.Level 2: Generation Testing (After Retrieval Works)Example:test_cases = [    {        &#8220;query&#8221;: &#8220;What&#8217;s the refund policy?&#8221;,        &#8220;context&#8221;: retrieved_docs,        &#8220;expected_answer&#8221;: &#8220;30 days, unused products...&#8221;,    }]for case in test_cases:    answer = rag_system.generate(case[&#8221;query&#8221;], case[&#8221;context&#8221;])        faithfulness = calculate_faithfulness(answer, case[&#8221;context&#8221;])    relevance = calculate_relevance(answer, case[&#8221;query&#8221;])        assert faithfulness >= 0.9, &#8220;Stop hallucinating challenge&#8221;    assert relevance >= 0.8, &#8220;Actually answer the question challenge&#8221;If these assertions fail: Your prompt is probably bad, your model is too small, or your context is confusing the model.Common failure modes:Faithfulness failing? Your prompt isn&#8217;t emphasizing &#8220;only use the context&#8221;Relevance failing? Your prompt isn&#8217;t clear about what the user wantsBoth failing? Start over with prompt engineeringLevel 3: Production Monitoring (Always On)Set it and forget it. Then remember it when things break.What to track:Faithfulness (daily)Answer Relevance (daily)RAGAS (daily)Latency p95 (hourly)User feedback (thumbs up/down)When to alert:Faithfulness drops >5%RAGAS drops >10%Latency p95 >5 secondsThumbs down rate increases >10%What to review:Sample 50-100 queries/week for human reviewMonthly deep dive into failure casesQuarterly full evaluation on test setThe first time your metrics drop and you catch it before users complain, you&#8217;ll thank yourself for setting this up.What Good Actually Looks Like (Complete Benchmarks)For a Production RAG System:Retrieval:Hit Rate@10: > 90% (find at least one relevant doc)Recall@5: > 75% (get most relevant docs)MRR: > 0.7 (relevant doc near top)NDCG@10: > 0.7 (good ranking overall)Generation:Faithfulness: > 92% (don&#8217;t make stuff up)Answer Relevance: > 0.8 (actually answer the question)Context Relevance: > 0.6 (don&#8217;t waste tokens)System:RAGAS: > 0.75 (overall quality)Latency p95: < 3 seconds (users will wait)User satisfaction: > 4.0/5 (they like it)If you&#8217;re below these numbers: Your system isn&#8217;t ready for production. Sorry. Fix what&#8217;s broken before you ship.If you&#8217;re at these numbers: Ship it. Monitor it. Iterate.If you&#8217;re way above these numbers: You&#8217;re either overfitting to your test set, or you have a tiny, easy use case. Both are fine. Just know which one.The Tooling That Actually Matters (By Budget)Broke Startup ($0/month):RAGAS - Generation metrics (Faithfulness, Answer Relevance, etc.)ranx - Retrieval metrics (NDCG, MRR, Recall, etc.)Chroma - Vector database for local devSentence-Transformers - Free embedding modelsGPT-4o-mini - LLM-as-judge for evaluation ($5-10/month in practice)Pizza + engineering team - Manual annotation of test setsGrowing Product ($100-500/month):RAGAS - Generation metricsranx - Retrieval metricsPinecone or Weaviate - Production vector database ($70+)OpenAI embeddings + GPT-4o-mini - Better embeddings, cheap evaluationLabel Studio - Self-hosted annotation toolTruLens - Visualization and debuggingEnterprise ($1000+/month):LangSmith - Full production monitoring and tracing ($200+)RAGAS + ranx - All your metricsPinecone/Weaviate - Scale tier vector DBOpenAI GPT-4o - Higher quality evaluationScale AI - Professional human annotation ($500+)W&B - Experiment tracking and dashboardsTL;DR for People Who Scrolled to the BottomStart here:pip install ragas ranxUse OpenAI GPT-4o-mini for LLM-as-judge (~$10/month)Build 50-100 test cases yourself (engineering team + weekend)Run evaluation locally with these toolsThen add: 5.
